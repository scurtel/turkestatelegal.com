# Buying Property in Turkey as a Foreigner: Legal Steps and Key Risks

Foreign nationals continue to explore Turkish real estate for lifestyle, investment, and immigration-related reasons. The purchase process is workable for most eligible buyers, but it is governed by land registry rules that differ from many home-country systems. Understanding legal steps before signing or paying reduces the risk of discovering a title defect, an unauthorised seller, or a contract that does not reflect how ownership is actually transferred.

This article outlines the legal framework, practical purchase sequence, and recurring risks foreign buyers encounter. It is informational guidance, not a substitute for review of your specific transaction.

## Introduction

Turkey permits foreign acquisition of immovable property within defined limits. Success depends less on finding a listing and more on verifying registry records, seller authority, and payment structure before commitment. Agents can help with search; independent legal review protects your position when documents are in Turkish and sellers may be motivated to close quickly.

## Can Foreigners Buy Property in Turkey?

Yes—subject to legal restrictions. The primary statute is the **Land Registry Law No. 2644**, particularly **Article 35**, which sets the framework for foreign nationals acquiring immovable property in Turkey. Most nationalities with qualifying diplomatic relations may register ownership, but eligibility is verified at the Land Registry when you apply for transfer—not when you pay a deposit.

Foreign individuals may acquire property up to statutory area limits and subject to district-level caps. Apartment buyers in urban markets rarely encounter area limits; buyers of large land parcels should confirm caps early. Article 35 also interacts with reciprocity practice—while many nationalities qualify, confirm your status before paying non-refundable deposits. For a broader eligibility overview, see [can foreigners buy property in Turkey](/articles/can-foreigners-buy-property-in-turkey).

## Legal Restrictions for Foreign Buyers

Restrictions protect security and land-use policy. Key limits include:

- **Military and security zones:** Foreign buyers generally cannot acquire property in prohibited military or security areas. The registry runs automated checks during transfer applications.
- **Border-adjacent zones:** Additional restrictions may apply near land borders.
- **Aggregate area limits:** Foreign individuals face caps on total land ownership nationally and within districts.
- **Property type rules:** Agricultural land and certain asset classes carry additional conditions.

Assumptions based on neighbourhood popularity do not replace a parcel-specific eligibility check. A property marketed to international buyers can still fail registry review if it sits in a restricted zone or exceeds applicable limits.

## Step-by-Step Property Purchase Process

A typical residential purchase follows this sequence:

1. **Identify property and request Tapu extract** — Confirm registered owner, parcel data, and visible encumbrances.
2. **Obtain Turkish tax number** — Required for registry and tax processes.
3. **Conduct due diligence** — Review title, debts, permits, and seller authority before large payments.
4. **Negotiate and sign preliminary contract** — Define price, payment schedule, and refund conditions.
5. **Arrange official valuation report** — Used for transfer tax calculations.
6. **Clear encumbrances if required** — Mortgages or liens often must be discharged before transfer.
7. **Attend Land Registry appointment** — Complete transfer tax payment and register ownership.

Remote buyers may use a scoped power of attorney; see [buying property in Turkey with power of attorney](/articles/buying-property-in-turkey-with-power-of-attorney).

## Title Deed Transfer at the Land Registry

**Title deed transfer is completed at the Land Registry** (Tapu ve Kadastro Genel Müdürlüğü). Until your name appears on the Tapu, you are not the registered owner—regardless of what a private contract or agent confirmation states.

At the appointment, the registry verifies foreign-buyer eligibility for that parcel, processes transfer tax, and registers the sale. Buyers and sellers (or authorised representatives) attend with required identification and documents. For appointment logistics, see [title deed transfer in Turkey for foreign buyers](/articles/title-deed-transfer-in-turkey-for-foreign-buyers).

## Why Due Diligence Matters

Due diligence should include review of:

- Current **title deed (Tapu)** and ownership chain
- **Mortgages, liens,** and registry annotations
- **Zoning status** and municipality records
- **Occupancy permit (iskan)** where relevant
- **Seller authority**—especially for heirs, companies, or proxy sellers

Skipping these checks exposes buyers to disputes that are costly to unwind. A structured checklist appears in [legal checks before buying property in Turkey](/articles/legal-checks-before-buying-property-in-turkey).

## Common Legal Risks for Foreign Buyers

Recurring problems include:

- Paying substantial deposits before Tapu review
- Relying on translated summaries instead of verified registry data
- Discovering seller lacks authority (incomplete inheritance, invalid POA)
- Unpermitted construction or missing iskan
- Under-declared purchase price creating tax and future resale issues
- Pressure to close quickly without independent counsel

**Preliminary contracts alone do not transfer ownership.** They allocate risk between parties but do not replace registry registration.

## Should You Sign a Preliminary Sales Contract?

Preliminary agreements are common and can be useful when drafted carefully. Before signing:

- Confirm refund triggers if title cannot transfer
- Define payment milestones linked to verified clearance steps
- Clarify who pays which taxes and fees
- Have counsel review Turkish text even if you receive an English summary

Avoid signing documents you do not understand. For contract-specific guidance, see [property sale contracts in Turkey](/articles/property-sale-contracts-in-turkey-foreign-buyers).

## Conclusion

Buying property in Turkey as a foreigner is legally possible for most eligible nationalities when the process respects Land Registry Law No. 2644 Article 35 and related restrictions. The safest path combines early eligibility checks, thorough due diligence, clear contracts, and transfer at the Land Registry—not reliance on informal assurances.

## FAQ

### Do I need a residence permit before buying?

No. Property purchase and residence status are separate processes, though ownership may support certain residence applications later.

### Can I buy in any Turkish city?

Generally yes for qualifying properties and nationalities, but military zones and area caps still apply to specific parcels.

### Is a notarised contract enough to prove ownership?

No. Registered ownership requires Tapu transfer at the Land Registry.

### Should I hire a Turkish property lawyer?

Most foreign buyers benefit from independent legal review before paying deposits or signing binding terms.
