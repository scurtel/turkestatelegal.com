# Legal Due Diligence Before Buying a House in Turkey

Legal due diligence is the structured review of a property and its seller *before* you sign a binding contract or transfer money. For foreign buyers purchasing a house in Turkey—whether a coastal villa, an Istanbul apartment, or a secondary-home in Anatolia—due diligence is the main defence against title defects, hidden debts, unapproved construction, and sales contracts that do not match how Turkish registries actually work.

This guide focuses on the *legal* workstream: evidence you should demand, checks your lawyer should run, and how to align payments with verified facts. It complements general market articles by putting verification first, not last.

## Why Due Diligence Matters More for Foreign Buyers

Domestic buyers often rely on local networks and language fluency. International purchasers face additional friction: unfamiliar document formats, translated contracts, remote closings, and agents who may be paid by the seller side. Due diligence closes that information gap.

Skipping it is expensive. Common outcomes include discovering a mortgage only at the Tapu office, learning that a “sea-view” extension was never permitted, or finding that the seller lacks authority because inheritance was not finalised. Each scenario can cost months and significant legal fees to unwind—if unwind is possible at all.

## Phase 1: Title Deed (Tapu) and Ownership Chain

Every house purchase should start with the Tapu and end with a clean transfer. Phase 1 confirms *who* owns *what* and whether the registry shows problems.

### Obtain a current Tapu extract

Your lawyer should pull an up-to-date record from the Land Registry and Cadastre Directorate. Compare parcel identifiers, address, share ratios (for co-owned units), and area figures to what is marketed.

### Verify seller identity and authority

The person signing must match the owner—or hold a power of attorney that explicitly covers sale, receipt of funds, and Tapu transfer for *this* property. In inheritance situations, confirm all heirs have consented or that court orders authorise the sale.

### Review annotations and encumbrances

Look for mortgages (ipotek), foreclosures, easements, expropriation notes, or citizenship-program holding annotations from a prior foreign owner. Each item needs a plan: discharge before transfer, price adjustment, or walk away.

### Foreign-buyer eligibility for the parcel

The registry will block transfers that violate military/security zones or foreign area caps. Do not assume a popular neighbourhood is automatically eligible—run the check on the specific title.

## Phase 2: Debts, Charges, and Municipal Compliance

A clean Tapu does not always mean a clean house. Phase 2 looks beyond ownership to obligations attached to the property or seller.

### Utility and service debts

Outstanding electricity, water, gas, or condominium debts can follow the property or block management handover. Request recent bills and clearance confirmations where available.

### Property tax and dues

Verify local property tax status and homeowners’ association (aidat) balances. Large arrears signal either neglect or dispute with management.

### Building permit and iskan (occupancy)

For houses—especially villas with pools, extensions, or guest units—confirm permitted construction and occupancy certificate status. Unpermitted structures can trigger demolition orders or block future resale.

### Zoning and land use

Rural or edge-of-town houses may face agricultural land rules or development restrictions relevant to foreigners. Do not rely on agent maps; legal zoning confirmation belongs in diligence.

## Phase 3: Contract Structure and Payment Discipline

Due diligence includes the *deal* itself: does the contract reflect what diligence found, and do payments track risk?

### Preliminary agreement terms

Key clauses cover total price, currency, deposit refund triggers, penalty for seller default, delivery date for off-plan elements, and who pays which taxes. Ambiguous “deposit non-refundable” language is a frequent dispute source.

### Escrow and bank transfer habits

Favour traceable bank transfers over cash. Match each tranche to a milestone: signed contract after basic Tapu review, interim payment after encumbrance clearance, balance at Tapu appointment.

### Valuation alignment

Turkey’s official valuation report affects transfer tax. The Tapu declaration should align with actual price and appraisal outcomes. Under-declaration creates tax exposure and can undermine later immigration filings tied to the same purchase.

### Representation and POA review

If you buy remotely, scrutinise power of attorney scope and expiry. Narrow authority causes Tapu delays; overly broad authority creates misuse risk.

## Phase 4: Developer and Off-Plan Houses

Buying a house before completion adds project-level diligence on top of title work.

### Developer track record and SPV structure

Identify the legal seller entity. Review prior project delivery, litigation history, and whether the unit you buy is tied to land the developer actually controls.

### Construction agreements

Off-plan contracts should define completion date, specification standards, remedy for delay, and what happens to your payments if licensing fails.

### Mortgage on land or units

Developers sometimes finance construction through project-level debt. Understand whether your unit could be affected and require discharge or buyer protections before large payments.

## Phase 5: Immigration and Tax Consequences (If Relevant)

Due diligence is not only about the house; it is about how the house fits your plans.

### Residence or citizenship linkage

If you buy partly to support a residence permit or citizenship application, confirm that the property type, price declaration, payment trail, and seller identity meet the separate rules for those programs—before you pay, not after Tapu.

### Rental intent

Landlords face income tax and possibly withholding obligations. Structure advice early if yield is part of your model.

### Cross-border reporting

Your home country may have disclosure rules for foreign real estate. Legal due diligence in Turkey does not replace tax counsel at home, but your Turkish lawyer should flag documents you may need later.

## Practical Timeline: What Happens Week by Week

While matters vary, a disciplined foreign buyer often follows a rhythm like this:

**Week 1:** Shortlist properties; request Tapu extracts and basic seller documents; lawyer flags disqualifying issues.

**Week 2:** Deep dive on encumbrances, permits, debts, and inheritance authority; negotiate contract marks-ups.

**Week 3:** Sign preliminary agreement with guarded deposit; continue clearance work for mortgages or liens.

**Week 4+:** Schedule Tapu appointment; arrange valuation report and tax payments; attend closing or execute POA closing.

Rushing this calendar because “another buyer is interested” is a common fraud and pressure tactic. Legitimate sellers accept reasonable verification time when the buyer is serious.

## Red Flags That Should Pause the Deal

Stop or escalate to senior counsel if you encounter:

- Seller refusal to provide Tapu or pushes for cash-only payment
- Price far below market with urgency to wire abroad
- Name on contract does not match Tapu owner without clear POA
- Major unpermitted construction presented as “already resolved verbally”
- Tapu area materially smaller than marketed living space without explanation
- Citizenship-marketed property sold by another foreigner without CBI eligibility review

## FAQ

### How long does legal due diligence take?

Simple resales with cooperative sellers often take one to three weeks. Complex inheritance, off-plan, or encumbered titles take longer. Do not book non-refundable travel for Tapu until counsel advises.

### Can I do due diligence myself?

You can read documents, but registry checks, legal Turkish terminology, and contract negotiation require local expertise. Most foreign buyers use a Turkish lawyer representing only their interests.

### Is due diligence different for apartments vs houses?

Houses add land, permit, and boundary issues; apartments add condominium bylaws, aidat, and shared building compliance. Both need Tapu and encumbrance review.

### When should I pay the deposit?

After initial Tapu review and clear contract terms on refund if title cannot transfer—unless you consciously accept higher risk for strategic reasons your lawyer documents.

### Does due diligence guarantee no problems?

No review eliminates all future risk, but it dramatically reduces preventable failures and strengthens remedies if the seller breaches.
